Taxonomy

Two families of tactile sensing

Every tactile sensor does one job: turn a physical contact into numbers. There are only two ways to do it — and the split decides everything downstream.

Optical

Photograph the contact

A camera inside the fingertip watches a soft gel deform against the object. Contact becomes an image, which is then processed into a height map.

  • ~megapixels of detail
  • 25–120 fps — camera-limited
  • 20–30 mm thick — needs a light path
  • gel is a consumable that wears
  • force is inferred, not measured
Non-visual

Measure the contact electrically

No camera. A material whose electrical property changes under pressure sits at the contact surface. Contact becomes numbers, directly.

  • tens to hundreds of points
  • 100–500 Hz
  • under 1 mm possible
  • no consumable — stable for months
  • force is measured directly
The trade

Optical buys spatial detail and pays in speed, bulk and wear. Non-visual buys speed, thinness and durability and pays in detail.

Part one — the two technologies

What each one actually does

One optical, one non-visual. The line that matters is the last one in each — how many numbers come out per sensing point.

Optical camera + gel

Three coloured LEDs light a soft gel from different angles. Press an object in and each surface slope reflects a different mix of colour; a camera underneath records it, and colour maps back to angle, which integrates into a height map of the contact.

Output — a depth map: heights in millimetres, computed from the camera frame. Geometry in extraordinary detail.

Non-viusal (Piezoresistive) resistance drops when squeezed

Carbon particles in soft rubber: press → particles crowd together → electricity flows easier → resistance falls. Copper strips crossing above and below turn one film into a grid, where every crossing is a sensing point.

Output — one integer per taxel. Pressure magnitude only, no direction.

Part two — Magnetic

A magnet floating on rubber

A third approach on a completely different principle — and the only one of the three that reports which direction a force pushes. A magnet sits in soft rubber above a three-axis Hall chip: press and it moves down, slide and it moves sideways, and the chip measures both separately.

1Press: rubber squashes, magnet drops closer to the chip → Z rises. Field strength climbs steeply — a 0.1 mm shift already gives a large signal.
2Slide: rubber shears, magnet moves sideways → Y rises while Z barely moves.
3Different motions land in different channels. In carbon film, sliding adds no compression so slip is never captured — here it's a separate number. That separation is why it catches slip.

Why it earns its own section

Optical and piezoresistive measure a single quantity at each point. Magnetic measures three — magnitude and direction. Output: three numbers per point.
